Introduction<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

The pragmatism of Richard Rorty sparked much attention amongst philosophers. Rorty\u2019s proposal is distinctive from many existing theories in philosophy as it does not require the presupposition (of the value) of truth. To be sure, Rorty rejects the notion of truth being a pre-determined conception that is waiting to be discovered by scientists and some philosophers1<\/a><\/sup>. For Rorty, the conventional understanding of truth is made<\/em>, rather than found<\/em>, and has \u201ctake(n) hold of the imagination of Europe.\u201d2<\/a><\/sup>. A distinction between fact and the description of fact ought to be taken seriously. The notion of truth applies only to the language that humans have created, for example, the description of fact; while fact itself is independent from the descriptions that we ascribe. That is, only upon statements constructed by languages can we assess its truth values, but neither our assessment nor the statement itself relates to facts. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

In a similar vein, Rorty famously claims, \u201ctake care of freedom and truth will take care of itself\u201d3<\/a><\/sup>; this extrapolates his attitude towards truth. For Rorty, our deliberate pursuit of our truth is constrained by our language. What is ultimately important, for him, is what matters in reality. As truth, according to Rorty, is made by languages and by extension, its user; its social position, therefore, is much dictated by whom utters the statement. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

Though insightful and interesting, I find the Rortian account to be indifferent in type<\/em> compared to the Rawlsian notion of the non-ideal theory. For Rawls, the non-ideal theory <\/p>\n\n\n\n

\u201c\u2026asks how this long-term goal might be achieved, or worked toward, usually in gradual steps. It looks for policies and courses of action that are morally permissible and politically possible as well as likely to be effective\u2026 non-ideal theory presupposes that ideal theory is already on hand.\u201d4<\/a><\/sup>5<\/a><\/sup><\/p><\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

At this point, it might be argued that the Rawlsian conceptions are unjustifiably deterministic, e.g., that non-ideal theory relies on (the existence) the ideal theory (which determines what it means to be ideal). But, as this essay argues, such interpretation (though reasonable), is not necessary nor sufficient. Moreover, in another equally reasonable conception of the non-ideal theory, Rortian theory is a coherent example of non-ideal theories. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

This essay proceeds in three steps. Firstly, I lay the groundwork by looking at the distinction between ideal\/non-ideal theory according to Rawls and show how Rawls\u2019s cannot escape from assumptions of truth. I explain how, for Rawls, is ideal integral to non-ideal. Then, I visit a free-standing non-ideal theory from Amartya Sen, which stands contrary to Rawls\u2019s distinction. I show how Rorty\u2019s theory6<\/a><\/sup> does not align with either Rawls\u2019s or Sen\u2019s theories. Finally, I qualify my claim by looking at Laura Valentini\u2019s three understandings of ideal\/non-ideal theory and show Rorty\u2019s theory encompasses characteristics from all distinct understandings qua Valentini\u2019s. With three understandings taken altogether, I argue, Rorty\u2019s theory is non-ideal in the Rawlsian sense as his theory leaves open for an ideal (e.g., Rawlsian). <\/p>\n\n\n\n

Ideal or Non-Ideal Theory?<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

The distinction between ideal and non-ideal theory is attributed to John Rawls. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

According to Rawls, ideal theory:<\/p>\n\n\n\n

\u201cassumes strict compliance and works out the principles that characterise a well-ordered society under favourable circumstances\u201d7<\/a><\/sup><\/p><\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

Recap on the definition of non-ideal theory, ideal theory, serves as a basis for non-ideal theory, as it already assumes that an ideal theory is \u201con-hand\u201d8<\/a><\/sup>. The ideal\/non-ideal dissection between theories is by no means unproblematic9<\/a><\/sup>. What is important for our discussion, however, is not<\/em> so much whether the Rawlsian conception of ideal is, in fact, unproblematic. Rather, we examine whether Rawls\u2019s ideal presupposes the any notion of truth. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

Firstly, we need to be clear about the Rawlsian strategy with idealising<\/em> justice or, put plainly, the procedure in which does Rawls derive any ideal reasonably. One intuitive strategy is the Original Position (OP) and the Veil of Ignorance (VI) 10<\/a><\/sup>. Roughly, OP is a thought experiment that provokes us, behind the VI, what sort of system would we want to be born into. Here, the purpose of the VI is to: \u201cexclude the knowledge of those contingencies which sets men at odds and allows them to be guided by their prejudices.\u201d11<\/a><\/sup>. Excluded knowledge, for example, includes one\u2019s sex, race, and culture12<\/a><\/sup>. For Rawls, a reasonable person will come to agree his two Principles of Justice (PJ) after deliberating in the OP13<\/a><\/sup>, where the first principle is lexically prior<\/em>14<\/a><\/sup> to the second. Here, we see Rawls\u2019s focus on liberty and equality15<\/a><\/sup>. Rawlsian theory also concerns the design of basic social institutions such that a just and stable16<\/a><\/sup> society can be formed based on the PJs. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

So far, I have yet to discuss \u201ctruth\u201d in Rawlsian theories. Nonetheless, it does seem that Rawls takes much for granted (e.g., that reasonable people necessarily<\/em> comply with PJ, and\/or their compliance is, in fact, strict<\/em>). For Rortians, it could be argued that Rawlsian theory requires<\/em> presuppositions of truth. For example, it is unclear what Rawls mean by \u201creasonable\u201d, and by extension, whether its conception (whatever it may be) is indeed shared by other members of society17<\/a><\/sup>. We might, therefore, be tempted to argue that Rawls is question-begging or is implicitly assuming truth to a certain extent. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

Rorty sees Rawls to be free from assuming a \u201c\u2018philosophical foundations\u2019 of liberalism\u201d18<\/a><\/sup> as Rawls claims to rejects a conception of justice \u201cnot<\/em> being true to an order antecedent and given to us\u2026\u201d19<\/a><\/sup>. To be sure, Rawls argues for procedural justice, the idea of which \u201cis to design the social system so that the outcome is just whatever it happens to be, at least so long as it is within a certain range.\u201d20<\/a><\/sup> He distinguishes between perfect and imperfect procedural justice, where perfect justice encapsulates 1) \u201can independent criterion\u2026defined separately from and prior to the procedure which is to be followed\u201d, and 2) that \u201cit is possible to devise a procedure that is sure to give the desired outcome.\u201d21<\/a><\/sup>; while imperfect procedural justice captures 1), but not 2). Rawls\u2019s attempt, however, seems to be unsatisfactory to bracket all contributing factors to the ranking of principles: for any<\/em> criterion, however independent, still hinges on individual preference22<\/a><\/sup>. In addition, that \u201ccertain range\u201d does not provide enough information can give rise to some pre-existing intuitions and values (e.g., truth) from people. If I am correct, then, procedural justice may still contain values that are arbitrary (e.g., truth)23<\/a><\/sup>. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

One way to overcome these reservations about assumptions on truth is to discharge the notion of ideal, according to Sen, <\/p>\n\n\n\n

\u201cA transcendental approach is neither necessary nor sufficient for answering questions on the advancement of justice that urgently demand our attention, which call for a robustly comparative approach.\u201d<\/em>24<\/a><\/sup>.25<\/a><\/sup><\/p><\/blockquote>\n\n\n\n

To clarify, a transcendental approach is not necessary as the mere existence of certain ideal does not entail people\u2019s compliance, nor is it sufficient as it cannot guarantee a complete framework of justice26<\/a><\/sup>. For Sen, a non-ideal theory is self-sufficient for we can rely on our intuitions while comparing between cases and judge which one is more just without requiring a pre-existing notion of justice. To tell which mountain is higher, knowing which mountain is the highest in the world (e.g., Mount Everest) does not help us to determine between the two mountains before us. Furthermore, we might add, a shared conception of \u201chigh\u201d might not be necessary either, as long as we understand the criteria with which we are comparing against (e.g., height, rather than, say, width)27<\/a><\/sup>. Sen\u2019s non-ideal approach is alike act-utilitarianism where the decision to undertake any action rests on the judgment in every case (i.e., which action yields more utility\/happiness). Act-utilitarianism stand in contrast with rule-utilitarianism where the preferred action is dictated by the rules (e.g., law). However, following act-utilitarianism might overlook the long-term benefits that we can otherwise preserve if we follow rule-utilitarianism. For example, speeding so that you can to the hospital early to see your injured family members trivialises traffic law for you; and when you break the traffic law more often, you are more likely to get harmed28<\/a><\/sup>. Complying with rule, on the other hand, might yield great benefits in the long term at some cost in the short term. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

However, elsewhere for Rorty, our reliance on moral intuition merely asserts<\/em> rather than explain what is \u201cgood\u201d. Also, what we might derive from philosophy or logic as \u201cgood\u201d, need not comply with what is in fact<\/em> good. 29<\/a><\/sup> Sen\u2019s theory, as we have seen, relies heavily on our intuitions. Therefore, Sen\u2019s proposal of a free-standing non-ideal theory is incompatible with Rortian theory. Nevertheless, what I have shown in this section, is that the Rortian theory is neither ideal<\/em> in the Rawlsian sense, nor is it non-ideal<\/em> in Sen\u2019s sense, which stand opposite of Rawlsian theory.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The In-Betweener<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

In this section, I will explore if there is ground in-between the two extreme positions30<\/a><\/sup> that is able to accommodate Rortian thoughts. For Valentini, the distinction between ideal\/non-ideal theory could be understood in three ways, respectively: 1. Full-compliance v. Partial-compliance; 2. Utopian\/Idealistic v. Realistic; and 3. End-state v. Transitional31<\/a><\/sup>. What I will show in this section, is while Rorty does not exclusively commit to any of these categories; but following Valentini, Rorty is prone be understood as non-ideal because his theory encompasses non-ideal characteristics from all<\/em> distinctions of non-ideal. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

As we see previously, Rawlsian ideal theory assumes full\/strict compliance. He believes that ideal theory is the ultimate values according to which we deal with social injustice. This perfectionist position is problematic, as I previously argued, as it can have footings on truth. For Rorty, that language is arbitrary makes the nature of truth<\/em> (a formulation by language) a non-profitable topic for philosophical pursuit32<\/a><\/sup>. Moreover, attempts to argue on the topic of nature requires language, that, as Rorty consistently argues, yield the process to be arbitrary. Recall, the physical existence of the world (e.g., fact), for Rorty, is independent of our description of it. The difference between Rawlsian and Rortian theory is that Rortian theory is not (at all) prescriptive33<\/a><\/sup>. Put differently, for Rorty, people\u2019s action is not determined by conceptions of justice agreed in society; people\u2019s motivation for action, thus, is not prescribed by any conception of justice34<\/a><\/sup>.35<\/a><\/sup> However, Rorty\u2019s theory does<\/em> require partial compliance, as he \u201c\u2026urges us to think in terms of practices\u2026\u201d36<\/a><\/sup>; even if a universalistic moral philosophy is impossible to be derived37<\/a><\/sup>. That is, even though Rorty\u2019s theory differs drastically from Rawls (namely of its lack of prescriptiveness), he nevertheless requires people\u2019s attention be drawn, necessarily<\/em>, to issues in practice; this yields Rorty\u2019s theory partially compliant. In addition, Rorty\u2019s exclusive focus on the reality affirms the component of realistic<\/em> according to Valentini. However, we might, alike Rawlsian critics, question the extent to which Rorty\u2019s theory is, in fact<\/em>, considering all factors in reality38<\/a><\/sup>. For example, in cases where people genuinely<\/em> believe in a set of principles of justice that ought to be universally valid-should there be enough of these people-it seems that their principles ought to be taken seriously, regardless of its (lacking of) truth values. If Rorty\u2019s concern is only in practice, then, he ought to take principles that people in reality already<\/em> subscribe to seriously. This section concludes that Rorty\u2019s theory is non-ideal in the first two of Valentini\u2019s understandings. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

Regarding Valentini\u2019s third distinction, the Ideal\/Non-Ideal distinction could also be seen as \u201cend-state and \u2018transnational\u201d39<\/a><\/sup>. What this means for non-ideal theory is that it \u201casks how this long-term goal might be achieved, or worked toward, usually in gradual steps\u201d40<\/a><\/sup>. In contrast, a theory is end-state when it specifies how a society can be fully<\/em> just41<\/a><\/sup>. While Valentini broadly agrees with Sen\u2019s argument that a end-state theory of justice is neither sufficient nor necessary, she also notes from Simmons that without an ideal theory, we cannot tell whether a certain unexplored action is permissible, nor can we know what counts as success42<\/a><\/sup> (for justice). Valentini\u2019s two points on Simmons can be applied to Rorty\u2019s theory. Indeed, Rorty\u2019s deliberate rejection of notions of truth disqualify any sort of ideal (theory), other than, say, freedom. What this means for Rorty is that there is no way in which we can tell we are moving in the \u201cright\u201d direction for sake of justice43<\/a><\/sup>44<\/a><\/sup>. Now, Rortians would undoubtedly dispute that any \u201cright\u201d way for justice, again, relies on certain notions of truth (as such, becomes unappealing). Nevertheless, that there is no way at all for us to review our progress by following Rorty is alarming. We might<\/em>, for example, extend Rorty\u2019s focus of freedom onto an ideal of its own, e.g., freedom-maximisation; and thus be able to assess our progress by seeing the extent to which we have achieved freedom (e.g., as a society). But this move cannot be taken by Rortians as mere consideration of freedom as an ideal violates freedom itself as it tampers with procedural neutrality (e.g., when people engage in conversations without pre-determined goals in mind). Nevertheless, as argued above, Rorty argues against the reliance on our intuitions in matters of political morality. Though unclear, we might see nevertheless see Rorty\u2019s theory not as far as Sen\u2019s, but still being close to Sen\u2019s than Rawls; for both of these theories see Rawlsian ideal neither necessary nor sufficient. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

Conclusion<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

It is obviously arguable that because<\/em> of Rorty\u2019s rejection of pre-determined notions of truth that any attempts on mapping Rorty\u2019s theory on the scale between ideal\/non-ideal theory is futile. I disagree: while it is true that some philosophers take ideal for social justice to mean an exact collection of goods that we ought to pursue, there are many on-going disagreements with regard to, exactly, which type of goods is worthy of pursuit. Philosophers need not (and some have not, e.g., Rawls) take truth (and\/or notions of) for granted; and by extension, formulate any theory of justice base on a particular conception of truth. An appeal to objectivism in moral theory need not entail any specific types of good (or pre-existing values including truth). Indeed, as Derek Parfit argues, utilitarianism, Scanlonian\/Rawlsian Contractualism, and Kantianism could be combined into what he calls the Triple Theory (TT): \u201cAn act is wrong just when such acts are disallowed by some principle that is optimistic, uniquely universally willable, and not reasonably rejectable.\u201d45<\/a><\/sup>. TT appeals to more than mere people\u2019s intuitions and necessarily provokes judgements that can yield conflicting outcomes, where to resolve them requires abandoning certain values of ours. That is, while it could be argued that TT still requires certain assessment based on people\u2019s value, which might make it possible for people\u2019s pre-existing values (including certain notions of truth that people subscribe to); but the mutually-conflicting nature of this trilemma is likely put all values under close scrutiny. In other words, even if<\/em> people subscribe to certain notion of truth, their decision in their trilemma discharges pre-existing truths. I believe the TT consistently answers to Rorty\u2019s worry on the arbitrariness of language; as some of our sensations, from, say, Kantianism, does not require language to aid our own understanding. Note, however, this essay is no place to ground Parfit\u2019s TT. I merely invoke TT to show that a theory of morality is possible without relying on truth that exists prior to deliberation of any sort. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

This essay argues that Rorty\u2019s theory can be considered as an example of Rawlsian Non-Ideal theory. Firstly, I laid out two extreme positions in the ideal\/non-ideal debate: Rawls and Sen\u2019s. For Rawls, ideal theory serves as the basis for non-ideal, and specifies the contents of justice; while for Sen, ideal theory is neither sufficient nor necessary for a plausible account of non-ideal theory. As I showed in this essay, Rorty\u2019s theory is neither of these positions. However, as I also showed, Rorty\u2019s theory remarkably resembles characteristics of non-ideal theory from all three understandings of Valentini on non-ideal theories. As I argue Rorty\u2019s theory cannot be Sen\u2019s, this means that Rorty\u2019s theory resides between Rawls\u2019s ideal and Sen\u2019s non-ideal theory. Before end, I also elicit a possible moral theory that can serve as an ideal for Rorty. <\/p>\n\n\n\n
